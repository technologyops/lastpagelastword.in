Last Page Last Word
 
by Krishnan V.Chari
Not A Laughing Matter

As there is a shortage of clean jokes (!), I make it a point to find printable jokes from around the world, including cyber world. Today most of the Rotary club bulletins have a jokes corner. Good jokes from these bulletins are reproduced in Rotary News. However the disturbing trend seen in some bulletins is that, they publish jokes about one community or the other. A joke involving the Casanova James Bond published in the bulletin of a club from the city of palaces, may even offend the religious sentiments of a community. Though such jokes may evoke laughter or a smile, certainly this will not keep the Rotarians of the community at the receiving end in good humour. While strongly denouncing such trends, I wonder why the District Governors do not frown upon such bulletins. We have strongly objected to such jokes while reviewing the bulletins, which were entered for the annual ‘best bulletin contest.’ If such jokes amongst people and especially amongst Rotarians, lead to misunderstanding, surely this is not a laughing matter.

Down Memory Lane

Attending Rotary International Conventions has been a habit of Rotarians from India. TWA (Trans World Airlines) operated a convention flight No.905 from Bombay to Paris in May 1953.

ELECTIONS AND ROTARY

Every month, I start off with a resolve not to write about elections (in Rotary). And every month some item trips me up. This time it is the US Presidential elections and the televised debate that put paid to my wish to abstinence!

How is it that Rotary with its roots in USA has adopted a set of rules and regulations for electing Governors, that is observed more in breach? Canvassing is prohibited as per rules, but is rampant to the extent of corruption in kind or money for setting up even the nominating committee. An informal debate between the candidates for Rotary positions is not the norm, but is the standard practice for the US Presidential contest.

It is time the Rotarians woke up and wrote in their suggestions for the next Council on Legislation, before the rot sets in.

You Said it

The urge to dash off a letter to the editor of a newspaper on any socially relevant topic, or any disturbing event including complaints against public utility services, is understandable. But if this trait lands one with an entry in the Guinness Book of Records, should it be commendable? Though I am not undermining the talent of writing or being honoured with a place in the hallowed book, most writers of letters to the editors seem to be interested only in seeing their names in print. In this case, a Rotarian couple, who have both entered the Guiness Book for writing the maximum number of letters to the editors of newspapers and magazines in a year, have desired that this achievement be publicised in this magazine. A quick search revealed that not one letter was received by us on any topic, particularly when we cater exclusively to Rotarians. This brings us to the point that letter writing must be encouraged and habitual letter writers can try and graduate to writers of articles, which will find more space in the press and can also benefit the readers. Incidentally, letters can be either bouquets or brickbats and if they are truely analytical, editors would only be too happy to publish them.

EDITOR’S NIGHTMARE

Reports sent in by the enthusiastic Rotarians from clubs do tell us about the work being done by the clubs in serving the community. But the composition of the reports leave a lot to be desired.

A report of a zonal seminar in a club bulletin caught my attention since the topic was literacy. Hoping to pick up some clues for the impending Presidential Celebration in Literacy, I read the report. Spread over two pages, the item was full of names of those who talked, welcomed, proposed thanks and questioned, but was deafeningly silent on what transpired, failing to mention even one quote of a speaker! There is something wrong here.
