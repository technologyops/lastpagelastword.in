Scent of nostalgia!

In her nine long years of her life, Malarvizhi has smelt it all. She remembers her first smell of the sea as a child. Her mother Kannagi used to carry her in her hip, helping her husband Kannan with the nets at night as he set out into the sea.

Smell of fresh catch of fish early in the morning collection for the market, smell of ‘meen curry’ as Kannagi cooked for the exhausted Kannan, strong smell of Karuvadu (dried fish) on the nets for a rainy day, above all these, the overpowering scent of her mother as she hugged her green sari.

Then, one day, the sea smelt strange. They ran as the sea level rose. Suddenly the giant waves lashed. The last words of Kannagi was ‘Aiyo! Malar Vizhi.’

Smell of rotten bodies. They identified Kannagi by her anklets. The smell of her mother’s body burning and she can smell the ‘meen curry’ now as she hugged her emotion drained father in the relief camp. But the smell is not the same. The smell of ‘meen curry’ has changed forever. The only consolation was the scent of her mother as Malarvizhi hugged her mother’s green sari, with nostalgia.

Where is the money?

Is there money out there for a good cause? The answer would appear to be a resounding yes. A leading English daily with headquarters in the Southern India (the region most affected by the tsunami last December) received an unprecedented Rs12.50 cr. (US $2.841 million), all in a space of just two months. If the cause touches the heart, the hands open up is the message. The contributors cut across the whole section of the society - individuals, corporate houses, school children and association of persons - who have given expression to their compassion and solidarity with those affected.

A noteworthy feature has been the scale of contributions made ‘online’. Around 28 per cent of the total contributions came in through the Internet with the US, the UK, Australia, Singapore, Canada and the United Arab Emirates in the forefront. There was also significant contribution online from India.
The Rotary leaders and the Rotarians may well draw a lesson from this namely: tell the people the work that needs to be done - health issues, eradicating illiteracy, vocational opportunities for the unemployed and people will contribute.

Ask and TAP, the door shall open!

HISTORY? What is it?

In a recent bulletin I chanced to come across a column by Rotarian C.Sivasubramaniam of Rotary club of Nagercoil Suburban, RI District 3210. Keen Rotarians must have come across his earlier books on Rotary.

Rtn. C.Sivasubramaniam says “I have requested all clubs through my editorial and article on “History Speaks” to send their history, But to my dismay I have not received the history of any club of our district”. He adds, “The writing of history, which is not an easy joke, requires the reconstruction of the past as nearly as it has really happened. The history of a Rotary club should bring out the events that highlight the achievements with some minimal reporting of members who made a research in the history of the club.”

An observer of Rotary may be left wondering whether history begins on July 1 and June 30 for many leaders of clubs / districts, looking at the way the lessons of past are ignored or the lack of consideration of the future when the baton is invariably passed on.

CS also mentions the necessity of history jocularly in this couplet. “Those who don’t study the past will repeat its errors. Those who study it will find other ways to err”. Here is a salute to Rotarian

C. Sivasubramaniam.
Yes. We at Rotary News are hoping to do our bit to record the history of Rotary in India .

Can we get some volunteers please?
