LAST PAGE LAST WORD
 
 
by Krishnan V.Chari

Fishing in tsunami waters...


Natural calamities are not uncommon. The pivotal role played by the NGOs is not uncommon either. But what made the agencies flabbergasted was the outpouring of individual contributions that followed the tsunami waves surge in the Indian Ocean. The Disaster Emergency Committee (DEC), formed by the people of UK, coordinating big charities’ tsunami appeals, raised 300 million Pounds (Rs.2640-crore).

If you thought that people do not want to contribute anymore, then the DEC’s act exploded that myth. Polls conducted in different parts of the world showed that people under thirty years of age contributed more to the needy, thus scoffed the idea that young persons are more interested in video games and other such trivial things than helping the needy.

During natural disasters, aid agencies are the first to reach the spot. Contrary to the convention, many private companies and individuals rushed to the tsunami affected areas and started rescue operations. Not only did they raise money but were also ready with relief measures within few hours of the devastation. By the time Rotary visited the affected areas, we had already missed the boat. There was no dearth of money or relief materials. The moral of the story is, that the voluntary organisations should evolve a disaster management methodology and ensure that they are able to rush to the spot and swing into action.

Many private organisations including the media set up tsunami relief funds and collected huge amounts and they managed to disburse the same to the district authorities within hours of the natural fury. Individually, the Rotarians of the affected areas have done a yeoman service, but collectively no impact was felt other than undertaking the rehabilitation work like providing makeshift tents and fishing boats to the affected fishermen. Maybe in days to come Rotarians will play the role.

A disturbing trend is these days are the increasing numbers of non-official bodies/agencies entering the field of relief and rehabilitation, each one seeking to carve out space for itself. Many of them are fishing in ‘Troubled Tsunami Waters.’ Their bona fides need to be verified, credibility established and numbers limited to ensure effective monitoring, implementation and the affected are not taken for a ride. It is here the relevance of reputed organisations like Rotary come into picture.

Better late than never, we Rotarians have to set up corpus funds and collect money from willing donors and utilise it at the first instance of any eventuality. If in Britain, the people could form a DEC and collect a whopping sum of Rs.2640-crore, the service organisations like Rotary can do much more. Although, Rotary has evolved from a fellowship club to a higher pedestal of social service organisation, we need to evolve a methodology to formulate a disaster management plan in order to live up to our words, ‘Service Above Self’.

What is news?


Our fellow Rotarians of Sri Lanka have a feeling that adequate coverage was not given about the tsunami devastation in Rotary News. We believe that this is not well founded as evidenced in the February and March 2005 issues. Adequate coverage of the tsunami affected areas of Sri Lanka, coastal areas of Tamil Nadu, Kerala, Andhra Pradesh and the Andaman Islands is our aim.

We are operating with a limited infrastructure, and depend upon Rotarians to keep us informed.

To be fair, we do receive a lot of information of service rendered by Rotarians but the news worthiness of many is a debatable point.

In the famous quote of John B. Bogart, ‘When a dog bites a man, that is not news. But if a man bites a dog, that is news.’

Let me assure our readers that we don’t want to bite, but we want to give you biting news.
