by Krishnan V.Chari
A Very Happy 2005
To All Readers Of This Column

Let me end the year 2004 with the fervent hope that the New Year 2005, which will bring us to the centennial year and month and day of Rotary’s existence, will be a better one.

If the readers detect a sense of despondency, they are right. After all, the Rotarians in India have received a jolt from The Rotary Foundation trustees during the last 4 months. More than anything else, the psych of most Rotarians has been hurt. But I am happy to note that the same Rotarians have set about setting things right. The efficiency with which eight RI Districts have cleared the clouds of cobwebs in their TRF grants is to be applauded. I cannot but feel that the other Districts need to pull up their socks. Wonder what is holding them back?!

New year resolutions!

It will be nice if three Resolutions can be adopted by every Rotarian for 2005:
  • I shall participate in ‘Every Rotarian, Every Year’ programme of The Rotary Foundation;
  • In the year 2005, I (along with my fellow Rotarians) shall strive to elect the district governor by consensus and not by an election;
  • I shall participate in at least one Rotary project to serve the community.
Of course, I would like to hear of other resolutions of Rotarians in the centennial year.

Impressions

Recently, I went along with my better half, of course, on a fellowship tour organised by a Rotary club. Mind you, the cost ran to two instalments of Rotary Foundation Sustaining Membership for the three-day fun. I must confess that the fellowship was extremely good and the opportunity for young Rotarians to interact with the sixty-plus provided ample opportunities to gain an insight into what will be the expectations of the leaders of tomorrow. So what is the rub? Somehow I did not get the impression that my fellow Rotarians were enthused by The Rotary Foundation or the advocates of TRF. The Rotary Foundation district officers are generally looked upon as ‘collectors’ rather than ‘facilitators’. In the centenary year, there appears to be a need to reinvent the advocacy of The Rotary Foundation.

Surrogate

Rotary rightly frowns upon cheque-book charity. This antipathy also extends to ‘surrogate’ projects, meaning, projects executed by others for which credit is sought by the Rotary clubs or individuals.

I wonder at the mindset of so-called educated persons occupying positions in the corporate world. Look at the liquor advertising. The Government has banned this in hoardings (bill boards), magazines and newspapers for right reasons. Yet, we witness the disgraceful act of the producers who advertise the same products with same name claiming that they are peddling sodas, glasses, et al. Don’t tell me that the highly paid copywriter’s hands are clean.

Are we transplanting the same culture into Rotary activities in interpreting the laws? Let us call a spade a spade, and not smirk when some one attempts to be too clever by half.

Accountability

In a recent interview, Mr. N.R.Narayana Murthy, Chairman, Infosys, was quoted as saying in response to challenges in India’s democracy, “Accountability is the greatest challenge. Our leaders are accountable to whom? They should be accountable to the people....We have 500 million illiterates, 700 million without sanitation. If this is not lack of accountability, what is?” The talking mirror in me was struck by the applicability of the saying to Rotary affairs too in the context of the District Elections and the Rotary Foundation affairs.

Are you going bald?

Do you know that a study has indicated that “Proportion of men earning more than $8000 per month who complain that they are losing their hair, is 95% while proportion of men earning less than $1000 per month who complain that they are losing their hair is 35% only?” (Source Humanscape: November 2000. Compilation by Anoop Babani). So why not give away some money for a good cause in Rotary and keep your hair?
