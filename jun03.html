Promises to keep By Krishnan V. Chari


War is a messy business.

In the end nobody wins a war. Everyone is a loser. The Iraq war that has just concluded, or at least one phase of it, is no different. A newspaper in the United Kingdom has published details of the war in the form of a balance sheet (as on 10 May 2003) which summarises the war very succinctly.

49 Days since launch of the war
2,500 Iraqi civilians believed dead
10,000 Iraqi combatants believed killed
105 US soldiers killed
32 British troops killed
10 Journalists killed, 2 missing
30,000 Bombing sorties by Allies
50 Cluster bombs dropped
17,000,000 Iraqis reliant on food aid
1 in 4 Iraqi children under 5 suffering from severe malnutrition
40,000 Baghdad residents reliant on Red Cross for water
40 Top ranking Iraqi leaders still at large including Saddam
400 Queries a day made to the Red Cross by Iraqis seeking loved ones
$55 Billion spent on the war by the US
$100 Billion – the estimated cost of rebuilding Iraq
55% - Bush’s approval rating before the war
72% - After the war
45% - Blair’s approval rating before the war
52% - After the war
0 Weapons of mass destruction found.

An authoritarian ruler has been driven out but the suffering of the Iraqis continues. Blockades and sanctions, it is now apparent, do not hurt the high and the mighty, but hits the men and women who are now literally on the streets.

Rotarians should now be somewhere out there providing the desperately needed relief. Rotary leadership did indicate that our role would come after the politics (!) of war. Are we out there now? This is the need of the hour.

For the naysayers who feel that this is politics and we should stay clear, I recall the beginning of a great agency called OXFAM. “In 1942, an Allied naval blockade aimed at the end of Nazi occupation of Greece was in place. But it also cut off the food supply to the civilians. 2,000 people were dying everyday in Athens and Piraeus. In Oxford, England, a small group of people began to raise money for helping those affected by the Greek famine.” (Of course 60 years since that day OXFAM is still getting through to many war-torn countries).

The Thief of Baghdad maybe just a nostalgic fairytale but it has many varied shades today.

Talking about humanitarian causes, I do not seem to have come across any reports of the work done by Rotarians in offering succour to the Kashmiri brethren who are in the midst of a different type of conflict. I do hope that I am wrong.

39 steps back in time

39 Steps was a successful thriller of the good old days directed by Alfred Hitchcock. And going back in time - 39 years to be exact - was in itself a thrill. I was back in the Lake District of England after a gap of 39 years. This is without doubt one of the most beautiful and scenic places on this earth. While in 1964 the scenery and the environs were raw, now with the passage of time the place has acquired a mature beauty without the degradation one would expect in these modern times.

A couple of things made a deep impression.

Lake District is William Wordsworth’s country. What is admirable is that this great poet and his sister felt that universal education was the path to poverty eradication, and gave away many of their properties to primary schools for the poor. This was over a 100 years ago!

The second impression relates to environment. Beatrix Potter is a well-known writer of children’s stories and the creator of Peter Rabbit and other characters. She lived in Victorian England where girls from wealthy families were not sent to regular schools but were groomed and taught by governesses at home. So this lady who did not attend a formal school wrote many stories that even today catch the imaginations of tiny tots around the world.

The wealth that Beatrix amassed in writing was put to use in acquiring properties in the Lake District and protecting the environment. At the end of her life she gave away all her properties - vast tracts of land and forests in the rural areas - to the people of England and these now form a part of the National Trust of England.

What is the message for us?

Just that people are the key to the development of humans and of nature, not some unknown faces in the government. These are shining examples for the Rotarians of South Asia.
